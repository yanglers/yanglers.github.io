A stream of consciousness

(Mar 2026)

I was born and raised in East Brunswick, New Jersey. It's admittedly not the most exciting place, nor is it somewhere that I return to as often as I should — but I still consider it my home. I trace the most fundamental parts about me to key memories I had back in that part of shrub-lined suburbia.

In middle school, I became deeply curious about our world beyond this shrub-lined suburbia: the countries of our world, the flags they raised, and how their people interacted with each other and those of other countries. I'd check out two or three atlases at a time from my school library and spent my bus rides to and from school reading through page after page. It helped that I was shy and introverted, so I preferred to spend these moments alone. This obsession has pervaded my life even up until now. There's an atlas that a friend gifted me for a Secret Santa that I keep under my bed that I read religiously before I sleep, and I have an affinity towards Atlas the Titan. I strive to have the ἀτλάς (endurance, tenacity) that he embodies.

In fact, "tenacity" is what the second character of my Chinese name, "毅" (yi), means. The other, "智" (zhi), most closely means wisdom. However, when you read my Chinese name in the Western style (first name followed by last name), "Yizhi Yang" becomes a homophone for "一只羊", or "one sheep". Because my Chinese name is my middle name, my full name thus becomes "Austin Yizhi Yang" or "Austin one sheep". I used to be embarrassed by this, but nowadays I find it really cute and embrace it fully 🐑.

Speaking of nicknames, a lot of my close friends call me "yanglers" (it's also the name of this website!). It's something that came to be sometime in the beginning of high school and something I've come to fully embrace, even through college. I would be lying if I claimed to have a profound backstory or meaning to the nickname. I actually used it to refer to myself in my roommate essay for my application to Stanford, and my admissions officer used it in her note addressed to me!

Stanford was actually the only school outside the East Coast that I applied to — I initially had no intention of leaving that part of the United States for college. In fact, move-in day freshman fall was my first time ever stepping foot in the state of California. However, looking back, I am glad that I went to school on the West Coast. Not only is the weather much nicer for a much larger portion of the year, but it's exposed me to a fundamentally different standard of living and thinking. I remember sometime in the first week of NSO (New Student Orientation), my freshman roommate (who is from California) was shocked at how fast I walked (I've since slowed down my own pace). I also like to believe that I've had a pretty noticeable glow-up in college, though if you've seen what I looked like in high school, you would know that this was not a high bar to pass.

More importantly, Stanford quickly gave me the belief that I was far more capable than I originally thought I was. With this belief came eustress — a motivation to work hard in college to get as asymptotically close to this potential as possible. I am grateful to my past self for developing this motivation early (though I certainly have reservations towards how I exercised that motivation at times). However, I can also say with confidence that I am a different person and have taken a different path than what I would have predicted freshman year — but I would also say that this was to be expected.

I had a vague "A to B to C to D" 4 year plan of how I wanted to go through college. I wanted to have this kind of opportunity that would lead to this other kind of opportunity that I would take that would lead to another and so on. But when you are at position B, you might realize that there is something else that seems more exciting than C, and you choose that instead. After I did that multiple times, I found myself at a Tatte in Cambridge (Massachusetts) enjoying a pistachio latte with the man who would become one of my closest mentors and my manager at F-Prime.

I would consider Cambridge (and Boston) to be my third home after East Brunswick and Stanford. I took my sophomore winter and spring off to work at Moderna, where I worked in the HQ at Kendall Square. I subletted a small room in Central at the time, so I'd religiously spend my afternoons and evenings crossing the Harvard Bridge to window shop on Newbury Street (especially the Uniqlo there) or taking the Red Line to frolic in Chinatown - there's this Hunan place that I would go to extremely often for dinner. I briefly worked in Amgen's Cambridge office when I would later work for them (though I was primarily remote and in New York City instead) and later worked in a life sciences consulting firm in downtown Boston, where I subletted a room in an MIT frat house in Fenway.

Nevertheless, I am at F-Prime now. I unfortunately have to be opaque about what exactly I do, but rest assured that I find my work exciting and am having a good time. I plan on staying here at least until I complete my master's degree at Stanford (though I will be taking a break to join Point72 this upcoming summer!). What's after that? I have no idea. If the juxtaposition between my frosh prediction of myself and my current position is any indication, then any prediction that I make now will likely be laughably inaccurate. Rather, I will continue making decisions that my present self believes are the most exciting and the best fit for myself, and we will see where that leads me.

And I will try my best to enjoy the ride to exploring our world beyond shrub-lined suburbia :)
