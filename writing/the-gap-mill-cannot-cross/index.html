The Gap Mill Cannot Cross

The most famous sentence in Chapter 4 of Utilitarianism is also its most notorious: "each person's happiness is a good to that person, and the general happiness, therefore, a good to the aggregate of all persons." Critics since G.E. Moore have attacked the word "desirable" in the paragraph before it, arguing that Mill illicitly equates what is desired with what deserves to be desired. This is a real problem, but it is not the central one. If we grant Mill the most charitable reading of his opening move, the proof still fails, and it fails at a deeper level than Moore's objection reaches.

The failure is structural. Mill is trying to derive an agent-neutral conclusion from agent-relative premises, and no logical inference can make that move without a normative premise that is simply absent from the proof. The gap between "my happiness is good for me" and "the general happiness is a good for all" is not a gap that more careful phrasing can close. It reflects a genuine conceptual difference between two kinds of value claims, and no accumulation of facts about what individuals desire can bridge it. Sidgwick saw this clearly, which is why he abandoned Mill's empirical approach entirely and simply postulated an axiom — but that move is instructive rather than satisfying, because it reveals exactly what kind of premise the proof would need and why Mill cannot derive it from the materials he uses.

Start with Moore's objection, because it needs to be set aside before the real argument can be made. Moore accused Mill of defining "desirable" as "capable of being desired" — treating the normative question of what ought to be valued as if it were settled by the empirical question of what is in fact valued. The analogy to visibility is meant to support this reading: just as something is visible if and only if it can be seen, something is desirable if and only if it can be desired.

The charitable reading of Mill does not support this interpretation. His point is evidentiary, not definitional. He is asking where we get our evidence about the good, not proposing a reduction of the good to the desired. If we want to know what the moral good consists in, the only available data is what reflective human beings, in conditions of full awareness, actually pursue. Desire is the faculty that registers value, not the criterion that constitutes it, just as sight registers the visible without constituting it. On this reading, Mill is not committing a naturalistic fallacy. He is making a methodological claim about how empirical inquiry into value must proceed.

This defense is at least coherent, and accepting it gets us to a genuinely plausible premise: each person's happiness is a good to that person. The egoist accepts this. Anyone who has experienced pleasure or the relief of suffering accepts it. It is as secure an empirical starting point as Mill could reasonably want. The problem is what he does with it.

The aggregation step commits the fallacy of composition: inferring that a property holds of a whole because it holds of each part. The inference "each part of this machine is light, therefore the machine is light" fails because lightness does not aggregate that way. Mill's inference fails for the same reason, and the failure goes deeper than a compositional error about weights or properties.

The premise establishes a series of agent-relative claims. An agent-relative value is one whose specification essentially refers to the agent for whom it is valuable: my happiness is good for me, your happiness is good for you. These claims carry built-in reference to a particular subject. The conclusion Mill draws is an agent-neutral claim: the general happiness is a good for all, meaning it provides reasons for action to any agent regardless of whose happiness is at stake. Agent-neutral value does not carry a subject-reference in this way. It is good from no particular point of view, or equivalently, from every point of view.

The logical gap between these two types of claim is not bridgeable by aggregation. Summing a set of agent-relative goods does not produce an agent-neutral good any more than summing a set of claims about what is true-for-me produces a claim about what is true simpliciter. The structure of the value claim changes in kind, not just in scope. An agent who accepts the entire series of premises — my happiness is good for me, your happiness is good for you, her happiness is good for her — has not thereby accepted any obligation to promote happiness that is not their own. They have accepted a series of facts about individual welfare, not a normative principle about how any rational agent should weigh competing interests.

The committed egoist can follow the proof step by step and conclude at the end that nothing has been shown to be good for them except their own happiness. This is not an unreasonable reading. It is the only reading that keeps the sense of "good" consistent throughout the argument. Mill needs "good" to shift from agent-relative in the premises to agent-neutral in the conclusion, and the proof provides no justification for this shift.

What would close the gap? The missing premise is something like: each agent has reasons to care about the welfare of others that are not reducible to their own welfare. This is a substantive normative claim, and there is no route to it from facts about what individuals desire. Desires are necessarily the desires of particular agents. Even a person who desires the happiness of others desires this as their own desire — it is an agent-relative fact about them. Universal benevolence as a psychological disposition does not generate agent-neutral reasons; it generates agent-relative reasons for those who happen to have it, which is not the same thing.

Sidgwick understood this, which is why his response to Mill is so revealing. He does not attempt to derive the impartiality of morality from facts about desire. Instead, he simply postulates it as a self-evident axiom of practical reason: "the good of any one individual is of no more importance, from the point of view of the Universe, than the good of any other." This is the Principle of Rational Benevolence, and Sidgwick is explicit that it cannot be proved from the premises Mill uses. It must be taken as foundational.

Sidgwick's approach is more logically honest than Mill's, but it does not rescue utilitarianism from the problem. It relocates the problem. Instead of a fallacious inference, we have an undefended axiom. The committed egoist who rejected Mill's conclusion can equally reject Sidgwick's postulate, and with more justification, since Sidgwick has at least been clear that it is a postulate rather than a derivation. The "point of view of the Universe" is exactly the kind of perspective that egoism denies is obligatory, and asserting it as self-evident does not make it so for someone who does not find it evident.

Both philosophers are trying to solve the same problem: how to move from the undeniable fact that individuals have interests to the normative claim that any rational agent should give those interests impartial weight. Mill tries to derive this through an empirical proof and fails. Sidgwick acknowledges it cannot be derived and asserts it directly. Neither route reaches an agent who is not already committed to some form of moral impartiality. The problem is not a fixable flaw in either argument; it is a constitutive difficulty in utilitarian moral theory, which requires agent-neutral value as its foundation but can only start from agent-relative facts about individual welfare.

The standard defenses of Mill do not resolve this difficulty. The appeal to social feelings — Mill's claim elsewhere that we are psychologically disposed to care about others — conflates motivation with justification. An agent who is psychologically disposed toward benevolence has an agent-relative reason to promote others' welfare, because they desire it. But this makes altruism a preference rather than an obligation, and it provides no reason for agents who lack this disposition. The proof was supposed to show that the general happiness is a good for all, not that it is a good for those who happen to be benevolent.

The revisionist reading that limits Mill's proof to a standard for public policy rather than individual morality avoids the logical problem by changing the subject. If the conclusion is only that social institutions should be organized to maximize aggregate welfare, the agent-neutral claim is smuggled in through the institutional context rather than derived from the premises. But then the proof is not establishing what Mill says it is establishing. He introduces it as a proof of the Greatest Happiness Principle as a standard for individual conduct. Restricting it to institutional design preserves logical coherence at the cost of proving something much weaker.

The proof's failure clarifies something important about the structure of utilitarian ethics. The Greatest Happiness Principle is not a theorem that can be derived from facts about what individuals value. It is a foundational commitment to a particular conception of practical reason, one that treats every person's welfare as making equal and impartial claims on every agent. This commitment cannot be earned through argument from premises that do not already contain it.

Mill's proof matters not because it succeeds but because its failure is instructive. It shows that the gap between agent-relative and agent-neutral value is not a gap that empirical observation of human desire can close. Closing it requires either a postulate, as Sidgwick proposes, or a prior commitment to impartiality that the proof is supposed to establish. The person who finds the Greatest Happiness Principle compelling does not find it so because of Chapter 4. They find it compelling because they are already committed to caring about all welfare impartially, and the principle articulates that commitment clearly. Mill hoped to show that this commitment was required by reason. The proof shows instead that reason, on its own, does not require it.
