Kant's Quiet Concession

Kant's ambition in the Groundwork is to show that immorality is a form of irrationality. Not imprudence, not wickedness, not weakness of will — but a logical failure, comparable to asserting a contradiction. If he can show this, morality gains the same authority as mathematics: its demands are binding on any rational being regardless of their desires, history, or circumstances. The lying promise is his primary demonstration. A person who lies to borrow money they never intend to repay, he argues, wills a maxim that cannot be universalized without self-contradiction, proving the action to be not merely wrong but incoherent.

I want to argue that this demonstration fails, and that Kant's later work implicitly acknowledges as much. The failure is not incidental — it runs through every step of the procedure. But the more interesting observation is what the failure reveals: the Groundwork's picture of moral psychology, in which the immoral agent simply makes a cognitive error, is quietly abandoned in the Metaphysics of Morals, where Kant introduces a distinction between Wille and Willkür that makes immorality a matter of free choice rather than bad reasoning. That revision is a concession, and tracing why it was necessary clarifies what the original argument got wrong.

The Formula of Universal Law instructs an agent to act only on maxims they can will to become universal laws. To test the lying promise, Kant formulates the maxim: when I believe myself in need of money, I shall borrow it and promise repayment even though I know this will never happen. He then asks whether this maxim can be universalized, and concludes it cannot, because if everyone made lying promises, the institution of promising would collapse, making the lie itself impossible — no one would believe the promise, so nothing would be gained by making it.

The first problem is that this argument never gets off the ground if we do not accept the maxim as Kant has formulated it. Before any universalization can be tested, the agent must translate a concrete action into an abstract principle, and Kant provides no procedure for doing this. The same action can be described at many levels of specificity: when I need money, I will lie; or when the only means to prevent serious harm is to deceive a lender who would otherwise refuse; or, absurdly, when I, Austin Yang, on a Tuesday, need fifty dollars. These generate entirely different universalization results. The last can be universalized without any institutional consequence because its conditions are so narrow they are never met. The second can be universalized without obviously destroying the institution of promising, since it applies only in circumstances of genuine moral urgency involving a specific kind of lender.

Kant simply helps himself to a maxim pitched at the level of generality that will produce the desired result. There is no a priori principle that fixes the correct description — only a pre-existing moral intuition about which features of the action are morally salient. This is circular. The Formula of Universal Law is supposed to generate moral knowledge; instead it requires moral knowledge to generate a testable input. Onora O'Neill has argued that maxims must be formulated at a level others could in principle share, but shareability is a social and psychological concept, not a logical one, and does not escape the circularity. Barbara Herman's solution — that morally well-raised agents possess rules of moral salience that guide correct formulation — is more candid about the circularity, but it effectively admits that pure reason is insufficient: you need a prior moral education to use the test correctly.

Setting aside the maxim problem, the contradiction Kant claims to find is not the kind of contradiction he needs. A genuine contradiction in conception would be analytically false — like a four-sided triangle. What Kant actually describes is a practical frustration: universalized lying promises would undermine the social institution that makes promises credible, defeating the liar's purpose. This is a self-defeating strategy, not a logical impossibility.

Christine Korsgaard acknowledges this and reframes it as a "practical contradiction": the lying promiser wills an end (the loan) and the precondition of that end (a functioning institution of promising) while also willing the destruction of that precondition. But a practical contradiction is a conflict of purposes, not a violation of the law of non-contradiction. A general whose battle plan is so loud it alerts the enemy has made a fatal strategic error, not a logical one. The distinction matters enormously for Kant's project, because the authority of logic is unconditional — no rational agent can accept a contradiction. The authority of practical rationality, by contrast, is conditional on having the relevant purposes. An agent who does not care about maintaining social institutions, or who is willing to free-ride on others' compliance, has not contradicted themselves in any sense that compels rational acknowledgment.

This is the gap the committed amoralist exploits. They can follow Kant's universalization thought experiment and conclude: you have shown that my success is parasitic on the moral conformity of others, which makes my action strategically astute rather than logically incoherent. The FUL gives no answer to this response, because it has not bridged the gap between instrumental reason — reasoning about how to achieve one's purposes — and moral reason, the standpoint of universal legislation. Kant presupposes that any rational agent is already committed to the moral point of view, but this is precisely what the argument is supposed to establish.

The contradiction test also relies covertly on consequentialist reasoning. The claim that promises would become "vain pretenses" if everyone lied is an empirical sociological prediction, not an a priori logical result. It depends on contingent assumptions about how people would respond. In a world of sufficiently gullible or forgetful agents, the prediction fails and the contradiction disappears. A principle derived from pure reason cannot be contingent on facts about human psychology. Kant has smuggled in the empirical considerations his project is designed to transcend.

The deeper problem is what the failures of the FUL reveal about the psychology the Groundwork implicitly assumes. In the early Kant, a free will and a will under the moral law are identical. Autonomy just is self-legislation through rational principle. On this picture, an immoral agent is like a person who affirms a contradiction: they are not fully exercising their rational freedom but somehow failing to reason correctly. The lying promiser is making an error they should be able to recognize and correct through better thinking.

The later Kant abandons this picture. In the Metaphysics of Morals and the Religion within the Bounds of Bare Reason, he introduces the distinction between Wille and Willkür. The Wille is the legislative faculty of pure practical reason, which issues the moral law and presents it to the agent. The Willkür is the executive faculty — the power to choose whether to comply with the law or act on inclination instead. The moral struggle takes place in the Willkür, not in the Wille.

This distinction does the work the Groundwork's picture could not. It allows Kant to account for moral failure without attributing it to irrationality. An agent with a corrupted Willkür understands what the moral law requires and chooses to prioritize self-love instead. They are not confused; they are defiant. This preserves moral responsibility — we can only hold agents responsible for failures that are genuinely their own, and the Willkür framework makes moral failure a matter of free choice rather than cognitive error. The lying promiser is not a faulty calculator but an agent who has freely subordinated the demands of universal reason to the demands of their own inclinations.

The revision is philosophically necessary but strategically costly. If immorality is a free choice rather than an error of reasoning, the Groundwork's central project — proving that the amoralist is irrational — fails. The amoralist walks away logically unscathed. The later Kant essentially concedes this by giving up the identity thesis between freedom and morality. He preserves the moral law's validity while quietly relinquishing its claim to be compelling on purely logical grounds.

There is also a structural irony in the Groundwork that the teleological reading makes visible. Kant presents his ethics as a clean break from Aristotelian teleology — morality is to be an ethics of pure formal duty, free from any reference to purposes or ends. Yet the FUL's contradiction test depends covertly on teleological assumptions. The reason universalized lying promises generate a "contradiction" is that the institution of promising has a function: to create credible commitments. Undermining that function is what makes the maxim fail the test. This is an argument from the purposiveness of a social institution, not from pure formal logic.

The teleology becomes explicit in the Formula of Humanity, where Kant says persons are "ends in themselves." This is an irreducibly teleological claim — it assigns persons a status as ultimate purposes that determines how they ought to be treated. The Kingdom of Ends, Kant's ideal moral community, is itself a telos: a state of affairs that the moral law is oriented toward realizing. The FUL's contradiction test is a clumsy formal proxy for the more direct insight that lying to a lender treats them as a mere means, subverting their capacity for rational consent and violating their status as an end. The Formula of Humanity captures this without the logical machinery and without pretending to derive a contradiction.

The conclusion suggested by all of this is not that Kantian ethics is mistaken but that its most defensible formulations are not the ones Kant offers in the Groundwork's lying promise example. The insight that persons ought never to be treated merely as means is genuinely powerful and does not require the fiction of a logical contradiction. The picture of moral failure as free defiance of a law one acknowledges, rather than as cognitive error, is more honest about the phenomenology of immorality and better suited to grounding moral responsibility. These insights are in Kant, but they are expressed most clearly where he has moved furthest from the ambition of the Groundwork — which is perhaps the most revealing thing about that ambition.
