The Wrong Contract

When CAR-T therapy was approved for relapsed or refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, it represented something genuinely new in oncology: a single infusion that achieves complete remission in a substantial fraction of patients who had exhausted every other option. The therapy costs roughly $450,000 per patient. The five-year survival data suggest that for patients who respond durably, this is not an expensive treatment for a chronic condition. It is a cure.

The question of how to pay for it is correspondingly novel. Fee-for-service payment was designed for a world where treatment is ongoing and costs accrue incrementally. It has no natural mechanism for a one-time intervention whose value materializes over a decade. The policy response has been to develop value-based payment contracts, which link manufacturer revenue to clinical outcomes, distribute financial risk between payers and manufacturers, and theoretically ensure that high prices are justified by durable benefit. These contracts are the right idea. But they are being written between the wrong parties, and that mismatch explains why access to CAR-T therapy in the United States remains far more restricted than clinical need would suggest.

The standard account of the CAR-T access problem focuses on the payer-manufacturer relationship. Payers face a large upfront cost with uncertain long-term payoff. Manufacturers know more about the therapy's likely durability than payers do. The proposed solution is an outcomes-based contract: the manufacturer charges full price but refunds a portion if the patient does not achieve or maintain remission at some defined timepoint. Risk is shared, information asymmetry is addressed, and the manufacturer has an incentive to develop therapies that actually work in the long run.

The logic is sound. The problem is that this contract does not touch the party that is actually restricting access. In the United States, CAR-T therapy is predominantly administered as an inpatient procedure. Hospitals serving as authorized treatment centers bear the cost of apheresis, lymphodepletion, the infusion itself, and weeks of post-infusion monitoring, plus the full expense of managing cytokine release syndrome and neurotoxicity in a small but significant fraction of patients. Under Medicare's inpatient prospective payment system, all of this is reimbursed via MS-DRG 018, a single bundled payment finalized at roughly $274,000 for fiscal year 2025.

The total cost of care for a Medicare CAR-T patient routinely exceeds $500,000, not counting the therapy itself. New Technology Add-on Payments and outlier payments partially close this gap, but the NTAP is capped, temporary, and subject to eligibility criteria that become harder to meet as more products enter the market. Outlier payments cover only 80% of costs above a fixed-loss threshold that rose sharply in recent years. The consequence is that hospitals administering CAR-T under Medicare inpatient rules lose money on the majority of cases. In fiscal year 2023, 66% of MS-DRG 018 cases qualified for outlier payments, a statistic that reflects not exceptional circumstances but routine inadequacy of the base rate.

This financial pressure is not a minor friction. It determines which hospitals invest in CAR-T capacity and which do not, which patients get referred to treatment centers and which receive palliative care instead, and how many patients can be treated before a hospital's CAR-T program becomes a financial liability its administration will not sustain. The provider-level access barrier operates independently of formal coverage policy. A payer can cover CAR-T comprehensively while the hospital that would administer it faces predictable losses on every Medicare case.

A well-designed outcomes-based contract between a payer and a manufacturer does nothing to address this. The rebate flows from manufacturer to payer. The hospital's cost structure is unchanged. The MS-DRG payment is unchanged. The financial calculus that leads hospital administrations to limit CAR-T capacity or restrict patient volume is unchanged. The contract is solving a real problem, just not the one that is binding on access.

The contrast with Medicare's outpatient payment system makes this visible. Under Medicare Part B, drugs administered in outpatient settings are reimbursed at Average Sales Price plus 6%. For a $450,000 CAR-T product, this generates roughly $27,000 in margin above acquisition cost before accounting for administration expenses, which are reimbursed separately under the Outpatient Prospective Payment System. The financial incentive to shift CAR-T administration outpatient is substantial, and some programs have done so as CAR-T toxicity management has improved. But the inpatient setting remains clinically necessary for many patients, and the inpatient payment problem remains unsolved.

The policy literature has noted this disparity without fully confronting its implications for how value-based payment should be structured. Most VBP frameworks in this space are designed as bilateral contracts: manufacturer agrees to rebate payer under specified conditions. Adding the provider as a third party to this arrangement would complicate contract design significantly. But there is a stronger case that the provider problem requires a separate policy fix rather than inclusion in a three-party VBP contract, specifically reform of the MS-DRG 018 base rate and NTAP structure. Continuing to design bilateral payer-manufacturer contracts while leaving the DRG inadequacy unaddressed is a bit like negotiating the price of a drug that the hospital cannot afford to administer.

The second structural obstacle to value-based payment is the Medicaid Best Price rule, and it creates a paradox that illustrates how a regulation designed to protect one population can harm another. The Medicaid Drug Rebate Program requires manufacturers to give Medicaid the lowest price available to any commercial customer. This is the Best Price requirement. Its intent is to ensure that Medicaid, which serves low-income populations and has limited negotiating leverage, benefits from competitive pricing achieved elsewhere in the market.

In a market with stable, transparent prices, this rule works as intended. In a market where manufacturers offer outcomes-contingent rebates to individual commercial payers, it creates a problem. Suppose a manufacturer agrees to refund 30% of the therapy cost if a specific patient does not achieve sustained remission at twelve months. For that patient, the effective price paid by the payer drops to 70% of list. Under a strict reading of the Best Price rule, this sets the manufacturer's Best Price at 70% of list, and Medicaid is owed the difference on every unit sold since the price was set.

The implication is that a manufacturer who enters a meaningful outcomes-based contract with a commercial payer risks triggering a Medicaid liability that could exceed the value of the contract many times over. This is not a theoretical concern. For rare therapies with small patient populations, a single large rebate triggered by treatment failure can reset Best Price in a way that costs more than the original sale. The rational response for manufacturers is to avoid substantial outcome-contingent rebates in commercial markets, which is precisely the opposite of the policy goal.

CMS has issued guidance attempting to create safe harbors for VBP arrangements, allowing manufacturers to report multiple Best Prices corresponding to different contract structures. This is an improvement, but stakeholders report persistent ambiguity about which arrangements qualify and how rebates should be reported when outcomes are measured across a multi-year contract. The consequence is that manufacturers remain cautious, and the most meaningful OBCs, those with large rebates tied to long-term outcomes, are rare. The contracts that actually exist tend to use modest rebates tied to short-term endpoints, which limits the degree to which they genuinely align payment with durable value.

These two problems, provider financial risk and Best Price constraints, have a common structure. Both are cases where a policy designed for a world of ongoing, incremental treatment interacts badly with a world of one-time curative intervention. The DRG system was designed to bundle the costs of an episode of care around a clinical event; it works tolerably for hip replacements and pneumonia, where episode costs are predictable and technology changes slowly. It was not designed for a therapy whose acquisition cost alone exceeds the base DRG rate by more than 50%. The Best Price rule was designed to protect Medicaid from being disadvantaged relative to commercial buyers; it works tolerably when prices are fixed and discounts are uniform. It was not designed for outcome-contingent pricing where the effective price varies by patient based on treatment success.

The economic modeling of VBP frameworks for CAR-T tends to confirm what these structural arguments suggest. Outcomes-based contracts improve cost-effectiveness ratios in simulation when they work as designed: when tracking is reliable, administrative costs are modest, and rebates are actually paid when outcomes are not met. The model is sensitive to all three of these assumptions. When tracking success falls below 50%, which is plausible given patient mobility across insurance plans and the absence of robust long-term registry infrastructure, the OBC advantage over a simple status quo payment erodes substantially. When administrative costs are high, simple installment payments that smooth budget impact without linking to outcomes may be more efficient in expectation. The theoretical appeal of OBCs is real; their practical advantage depends on implementation conditions that do not currently exist at scale.

Installment payments address a different problem, the immediate budget shock of a $450,000 acquisition cost, without addressing value alignment. A payer that spreads this cost over five annual installments faces a more manageable cash flow but the same uncertainty about whether the therapy will remain effective over that period. For large commercial payers with stable enrollment, installments provide limited benefit since they can manage the cash flow internally. For smaller payers and Medicaid programs with high patient turnover, installments create a different version of the Best Price problem: the first installment may be treated as the full price, setting an artificially low Best Price before the manufacturer has received adequate compensation.

The argument for a hybrid model, where installment payments are contingent on continued clinical response, is in some ways the most coherent response to the structure of the problem. It aligns payment timing with benefit accrual, distributes financial risk across years, and creates an outcome-linked mechanism that is easier to administer than a lump-sum rebate triggered by a binary endpoint. The patient either remains in remission or does not; subsequent payments either proceed or do not. There is no large retroactive adjustment of the kind that triggers Best Price concerns.

In simulation, performance-based annuities compare favorably to both status quo payment and simple installments on cost-effectiveness grounds, while offering better budget impact management than an upfront OBC rebate. The practical barriers are real but different in character from those facing lump-sum OBCs. Patient tracking is still required, but the relevant question is simpler: is this patient still alive and in remission? This is more tractable than the attributable outcome measurements that some OBC designs require. The patient mobility problem remains, but its consequences are different: a patient who switches insurance plans mid-installment creates administrative complexity rather than a large contested rebate.

None of this resolves the provider financial risk problem, which sits outside the payer-manufacturer contract structure entirely. The strongest version of the argument here is that reform of MS-DRG 018 and the NTAP structure is a prerequisite for value-based payment to achieve its access goals, not a complement to it. A hospital that loses money on every Medicare inpatient CAR-T case has no capacity to participate meaningfully in a VBP framework, however well-designed, because the VBP is negotiated between the payer and the manufacturer while the hospital bears costs that neither contract party is absorbing. Addressing this requires CMS action on inpatient payment rates, not better contract design between manufacturers and payers.

The broader implication is that the VBP literature for high-cost curative therapies has somewhat inverted the causal priority of these problems. Most of the design attention goes to payer-manufacturer contracts because that is where the economics literature on value-based pricing applies most cleanly, and because manufacturers and payers are the parties with the negotiating infrastructure to implement complex contracts. But the binding constraint on access, for Medicare patients in particular, is not the absence of a good payer-manufacturer contract. It is a DRG that makes hospitals lose money administering the therapy and a Best Price rule that makes manufacturers cautious about the contracts that would most credibly align incentives.

Fixing the first problem requires a regulatory intervention that CMS has so far made incrementally and inadequately. Fixing the second requires either legislative reform of the Best Price calculation for outcome-contingent arrangements or a sufficiently clear safe harbor that manufacturers are willing to offer meaningful rebates without liability exposure. Both are policy problems, not contract design problems, and framing them as the latter has allowed the field to generate substantial modeling literature on VBP frameworks whose practical implementation is blocked by obstacles that better contract design cannot address.
