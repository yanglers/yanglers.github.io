A Fair Coin and Conservativeness

Hartry Field's nominalism rests on a bet: that mathematics is a tool, not a discovery. When we add math to a theory about the physical world, we should not end up saying anything new about that world. The theory should be the same theory, just more conveniently expressed. Field calls this conservativeness — and it is the heart of what makes his project attractive. If math is genuinely conservative, then the philosopher who wants to avoid mysterious abstract objects gets to keep everything science actually needs, at no extra ontological cost.

Probability is a good place to stress-test this bet. In science, probability does not merely list possible outcomes. It tells us what to expect, what counts as a surprise, and when a result calls for an explanation. A coin that lands heads forty times in a row demands a story. A coin that lands heads roughly half the time does not. The mathematics of probability seems to be doing real work here — not just representing something we already knew, but actually sorting outcomes into the expected and the exceptional. If that sorting is genuinely mathematical, Field has a problem.

The fair coin is a useful test case precisely because "fair" is meant to rule out any underlying bias. A fair coin, by definition, gives heads and tails equal standing. Nothing in the physical setup privileges one over the other. So if probability theory ends up telling us something about what a fair coin is likely to do, we might wonder whether that is really just math talking — or whether the math is, in some sense, reaching into the world.

Field's requirement is precise. Take a non-mathematical theory N — a description of the physical world stated entirely without reference to numbers, sets, or functions. Add a mathematical theory M — say, standard probability theory. Conservativeness says: any purely physical conclusion you can derive from N + M together, you could already derive from N alone. Math should not smuggle in new facts about coins, planets, or people.

The fair-coin setup, stated carefully, does not actually violate this. Here is why.

Suppose we have a physical description of a coin-flipping device — call it Ndyn. It says what the coin is made of, how it is flipped, what forces act on it. Now we add a probability model that assigns a half-half distribution to each toss, independently. From this, we can derive something like a concentration bound: for large n, the probability that the frequency of heads stays close to one-half is very high. More precisely, for any tolerance ε > 0:

concentration bound
P(|Sn/n − 1/2| < ε) ≥ 1 − δ

where δ shrinks as n grows. This is a theorem. But notice what it says: it says something about a measure on sequences of outcomes. It says a certain set of histories has high probability. It does not say that any particular run of this coin will stay near balance. To get from "this set has measure close to one" to "a normal run of this coin will land near balance," we need something more — a bridge that connects high probability to ordinary claims about what actually happens.

That bridge is not mathematics. It is an additional assumption: that high-probability events are what we should expect to see, or that they characterize what counts as a normal run. Without that assumption, the probability theorem floats free of any particular prediction. And that assumption — call it something like "high probability licenses expectation" — is not part of the mathematical theory itself. It is a linking principle that has to be added by hand.

This is why conservativeness survives. The probability theory, taken on its own, does not entail any new non-mathematical claim about what the coin will do. To get a claim about the coin, you need extra resources that are not mathematical in Field's sense. No purely physical conclusion follows from Ndyn + probability theory that could not already follow from Ndyn plus those extra resources alone.

So conservativeness is safe. The harder question is whether Field can still say, with a straight face, that mathematics is only a representational tool here — that it contributes nothing of its own to the structure of probabilistic science.

The trouble is that the bridge has to come from somewhere. If we want to say that a normal run of a fair coin will land near balance, we need a way of sorting the physically possible histories into the normal and the abnormal. Either we treat this as an epistemic norm — a rule about how to form credences — or we build it into the physical description itself, by adding something like a typicality predicate or a chance structure to the non-mathematical base.

Both routes work, in the sense that they preserve conservativeness. But both routes require the Field-style nominalist to supply, as part of the non-mathematical base, a substantive sorting of possibilities. And in a fair, symmetric case, that sorting is not free.

Here is the problem with symmetry. Suppose the coin-flipping device is deterministic: given the exact initial state of the device, the sequence of outcomes is fixed. Let I be the space of all physically admissible initial states — all the ways you might release the coin. Because the setup is fair, there is a symmetry that maps each initial state to a "mirror" initial state that would produce the head/tail-flipped outcome sequence. So heads and tails are treated evenly.

But to say that "most" initial states lead to near-balance, we need a weighting over I. Symmetry does not give us one. There are many ways to weight I that all respect the head/tail symmetry — all treating heads and tails evenly — while disagreeing sharply about what is typical. One weighting might spread probability evenly across all initial states and conclude that near-balance is overwhelmingly typical. Another might concentrate weight on a narrow band of initial states that happen to produce imbalanced sequences and conclude that imbalance is what to expect.

Symmetry rules out weightings that privilege heads over tails. It does not rule out weightings that privilege some initial states over others — and different choices here can reverse what counts as typical. The Fieldian who says "most initial conditions lead to near-balance" has quietly made a choice that is not forced by the physics.

A natural reply is that real coin-flipping devices are noisy. The initial state is never perfectly known; small imprecisions wash out. In that setting, the physics itself might seem to select a natural weighting — one that reflects the spread of our ignorance about the initial conditions rather than anything stipulated.

This reply has real force in practice. But it does not dissolve the worry. To extract a privileged weighting from noise, you have to add to the physical description: assumptions about the distribution of imprecisions, a model of how error propagates, a choice of which invariances matter. That is a substantive enrichment of the non-mathematical base, not something that falls out of the minimal fair dynamics. And even after you have a privileged weighting over initial conditions, you still need the bridge — the principle that says a measure-near-one set is what we should expect to see.

The same issue appears in statistical mechanics, where typicality talk is used to explain why gases expand and entropy increases even though the underlying dynamics are reversible. The idea is that the initial conditions of our universe are typical with respect to the natural measure on phase space, and typical initial conditions lead to entropy increase. This is a powerful and fruitful way of thinking. But it relies on a claim about which measure is natural — and that claim is not delivered by the dynamics alone. It has to be justified separately, and different choices of measure can license very different claims about what is typical.

None of this refutes Field. Conservativeness, as a formal claim, survives the fair-coin case. Probability theorems give facts about measures, not about what any particular coin will do, and so mathematics alone does not smuggle in new physical conclusions.

What changes is the character of the nominalist promise. Field's picture is attractive because it suggests that we can do science with a lean physical base, using mathematics purely as a representational convenience — a way of saying things we could already say without it, just more efficiently. The fair-coin case shows that probabilistic science does not quite fit this picture. To do the work probability actually does in science — distinguishing the expected from the exceptional, licensing predictions, underwriting explanations — we need a sorting of possibilities that the dynamics, on their own, do not supply.

Mathematics may still be conservative. But in the fair-coin setting, the non-mathematical base has to carry more weight than the original promise implied. The sorting has to live somewhere: in a typicality structure, in a privileged weighting over initial conditions, or in epistemic norms about how to form credences. On any of these routes, the clean picture of math as a transparent representational layer starts to look harder to maintain — not because conservativeness fails, but because the work that probability does in science turns out to require more from the nominalist's base than bare physical dynamics can provide.
