Prompting for Bioactivity

Molecular language models trained on SMILES strings can generate chemically valid, structurally novel molecules at scale. The open question is how to direct that generation toward molecules with desired properties without retraining the model for each target. The standard approach is iterative: generate candidates, score them, fine-tune the model on high-scoring examples, repeat. This works, but it is expensive — both computationally and in terms of time — and it produces a separate model for every target, which does not scale well when the target space is large or when resources are limited.

The approach we explore here is different. Rather than updating the model, we ask whether the prompt alone can do the steering. The hypothesis is that a pre-trained generative transformer, prompted with molecules whose structural similarity to other compounds is strongly predictive of those compounds' bioactivity, will generate candidates that are, on average, more bioactive than molecules prompted randomly or from the full assay. If this holds, the same base model can be redirected to any target by changing only the input, with no retraining required.

The theoretical motivation comes from the Similarity Property Principle, a well-established empirical regularity in medicinal chemistry: structurally similar molecules tend to share biological activity. This is the basis for analoguing campaigns in drug discovery, where chemists synthesize variants of a known active compound. The question is whether the principle can be operationalized computationally to identify, within a given assay, which specific molecules are the best structural anchors for generative exploration.

Standard approaches to this question ask: which molecules in the assay have the highest measured bioactivity? The prompt would then consist of the top actives. This is reasonable, but it conflates two different things. A molecule with high measured bioactivity is not necessarily a molecule whose neighborhood in chemical space is enriched for bioactivity. A potent compound might be structurally unique, sitting in a sparse region of chemical space where small perturbations produce inactive molecules. What we actually want for prompt-based generation are molecules whose structural context is informative about bioactivity across the assay: molecules such that knowing similarity to them predicts the activity of other compounds.

The distinction matters because a generative model prompted with a molecule will explore the neighborhood around that molecule in chemical space. If the neighborhood is bioactivity-enriched, the generated candidates are more likely to be active. If the neighborhood is sparse or unstructured with respect to activity, the prompt provides weak guidance regardless of the activity of the seed molecule itself.

The pipeline identifies these structurally informative molecules using a Random Forest regressor trained on pairwise molecular similarity. Given a ChEMBL assay of n molecules against a target of interest, we compute an n×n Tanimoto similarity matrix using Morgan fingerprints, where entry (i, j) represents the fingerprint similarity between molecules i and j. Each row of this matrix becomes a feature vector for molecule i, and the target is the vector of measured bioactivities (log-transformed Kd or IC50 values).

A Random Forest regressor trained on this representation learns which molecules' similarity profiles are predictive of bioactivity. Feature importance analysis on the fitted model produces Gini importance scores for each molecule, reflecting how much variance in bioactivity the model attributes to similarity to that molecule across all splitting decisions. The top-scoring molecules are the ones whose structural neighborhood is most informative about activity in the assay. These are the prompt molecules.

This framing has a specific advantage over selecting by raw bioactivity. It is explicitly relational: it asks not "how active is this molecule?" but "how predictive is this molecule's structural context for activity across the assay?" A molecule can rank highly on this criterion while having moderate measured activity, if its neighbors cluster strongly by activity level. Conversely, a highly active but structurally isolated molecule would rank poorly, because similarity to it does not carry information about the activity of other compounds.

The case study target is CBL-B, an E3 ubiquitin ligase involved in the downregulation of T cell receptor signaling. CBL-B inhibition is a target of interest in immuno-oncology: blocking it can sustain T cell activation and enhance anti-tumor immunity. We obtained two independent ChEMBL assays for CBL-B, fit separate Random Forest models to each (Model 1 and Model 2), and used the top-importance molecules from Model 1 as prompts to a pre-trained GPT to generate novel SMILES candidates. Generated structures were filtered for chemical validity using RDKit.

The evaluation design uses cross-assay prediction as a check against overfitting. Model 1 was trained on Assay 1 and used to select prompt molecules; the generated candidates were then scored by both Model 1 and Model 2 independently. Comparing the average predicted bioactivity of generated candidates against the average measured bioactivity of the original assay molecules under both models tests whether the improvement generalizes beyond the model used to select the prompts.

The generated candidates score higher than the Assay 1 baseline under Model 1, with a p-value of 1.46 × 10⁻³⁵. They also score higher than the Assay 2 baseline under Model 2, and higher than the Model 2 predictions for the original Assay 1 molecules, both with p-values below 10⁻³⁰⁰. The cross-assay result is the more meaningful of these: the fact that candidates selected using Model 1 score higher than the original assay molecules under Model 2, which was trained on an entirely different set of measurements, suggests the improvement is not an artifact of the selection model. The candidates occupy a region of chemical space that a second independent bioactivity model also regards as favorable.

Several limitations are worth stating directly. The bioactivity predictions are model-based, not experimentally measured. The validation chain is: Random Forest trained on assay data, used to score generated molecules, compared against the same assay data. A candidate that scores well under this pipeline may still fail in a binding assay or a cell-based assay, for reasons that neither the fingerprint similarity features nor the Random Forest model captures. Pharmacokinetic properties, synthetic accessibility, and off-target effects are not considered at any stage of the current pipeline.

The cross-assay validation partially addresses the circularity concern, but both assays measure the same target, so they are not fully independent sources of evidence. A more stringent test would involve generating candidates, having them synthesized, and measuring their activity experimentally. The p-values reported here reflect a statistical comparison within the space of model predictions, not an empirical claim about in vitro or in vivo activity.

The circular risk in feature importance is also worth noting. The Gini importance score for molecule i reflects how much the Random Forest uses similarity to i when predicting bioactivity in the training assay. High-importance molecules are those whose structural neighborhood is well-represented in the assay and whose similarity profile correlates with the target variable. If the assay itself has sampling biases, those biases will be reflected in which molecules rank highly. A structurally underrepresented region of chemical space with high activity would produce no high-importance molecules, even if it is biologically significant.

The core contribution is the use of Random Forest feature importance on a pairwise similarity matrix as a method for identifying structurally informative prompt molecules, as distinct from simply selecting high-activity compounds. This distinction has practical consequences: the selected molecules are anchors in activity-predictive regions of chemical space, not just the highest-activity compounds observed in a particular assay. Whether this translates to experimental activity in synthesized candidates is the question that motivates the next stage of the work.
