Competition Without Transparency

When a generic drug enters the market, the story is simple. The generic is chemically identical to the brand, it launches at a steep discount, pharmacists substitute it automatically, and prices fall. The brand drug loses most of its volume. Patients and payers save money. The mechanism is transparent and the outcome is reliably competitive.

Biosimilars were supposed to do the same thing for biologics, the large-molecule drugs that have come to dominate pharmaceutical spending. Biologic medicines now account for a disproportionate share of drug costs in the US, with a handful of top products consuming nearly a fifth of Medicare Part D gross spending. The Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act of 2009 created an abbreviated approval pathway for biosimilars, explicitly modeled on the generic pathway. The expectation was analogous competition, analogous savings.

The expectation has not been met, and the reasons why are genuinely interesting. The biosimilar market has not failed because competitors are absent. As of early 2025, the FDA had approved over sixty biosimilars across numerous reference products, with substantial market growth projected. The failure is more specific: the competitive mechanism itself works differently, in ways that are worth understanding carefully.

The first relevant difference between biosimilars and generics is technical. A small molecule generic is an exact chemical copy. Its active ingredient is identical to the brand's at the molecular level, and identity can be demonstrated analytically. A biosimilar is a biological product derived from living cells, and because biological manufacturing processes are inherently variable, no two batches of any biologic are perfectly identical, including batches of the originator product itself. Biosimilars are not exact copies; they are highly similar products with no clinically meaningful differences in safety, purity, or potency. This is a regulatory standard, not a chemical identity claim.

The distinction matters because it creates space for perceived differentiation where no real differentiation exists. Physicians and patients who understand the generic framework intuitively may apply it incorrectly to biosimilars, treating "highly similar" as less reassuring than "identical." Surveys consistently show substantial provider knowledge gaps: many physicians express concern about subtle efficacy differences, immunogenicity risk on switching, and the implications of the term "biosimilar" itself. These concerns are not supported by clinical evidence, but they shape prescribing behavior. A product that is clinically equivalent but perceived as inferior competes at a disadvantage that has nothing to do with its actual properties.

This perceived differentiation is the first thing the originator manufacturer can exploit. Standard models of market competition with differentiated products predict that when a new entrant is perceived as lower quality, the incumbent can maintain a price premium and retain a loyal segment of the market even under competitive pressure. The relevant question for the originator is not whether to compete, but how. And the answer the originator has consistently chosen is surprising: compete on net price, not on list price, and use the rebate system to do it.

To understand what this means, it helps to understand how drug prices actually work in the US market. There are two prices that matter. The list price, called the Wholesale Acquisition Cost or WAC, is the nominal price at which a manufacturer sells a drug to distributors. The net price, often measured as Average Sales Price or ASP, is what manufacturers actually receive after rebates and discounts paid to pharmacy benefit managers, insurers, and other intermediaries. The spread between these two numbers can be enormous, and it has been growing.

Pharmacy benefit managers, or PBMs, are the intermediaries who negotiate drug benefits on behalf of health plans. They design formularies that determine which drugs are covered at which tier of cost-sharing, and they negotiate rebates from manufacturers in exchange for favorable placement. A drug on the preferred tier is accessible to patients at lower out-of-pocket cost; a drug on a non-preferred tier or excluded from the formulary altogether faces a substantial barrier to uptake regardless of its clinical profile. Formulary placement is, in practice, the most important determinant of market share for many drugs.

When biosimilar competition arrives, the originator faces a choice. It can lower its list price and accept reduced margins, hoping to retain volume. Or it can maintain its list price and offer PBMs larger rebates in exchange for continued preferred formulary status, effectively lowering its net price while keeping its WAC intact. Empirical data on originator responses to biosimilar entry shows that the second strategy has been overwhelmingly preferred. In response to competition for drugs like pegfilgrastim, infliximab, and rituximab, originators increased rebates by 51%, 110%, and 257% respectively, achieving net price reductions of 47-64% with minimal changes to list prices.

The logic is straightforward. Lowering the list price is a permanent, visible concession that benefits everyone who buys the drug, including the price-sensitive segment the biosimilar is targeting. Offering rebates to PBMs targets the formulary placement mechanism directly, rewarding the intermediaries who control market access while leaving list prices high. For PBMs whose business model benefits from rebate volume, a high-WAC drug with large rebates can be preferable to a low-WAC drug with small rebates, even if the net cost is identical or higher. The originator's rebate strategy works precisely because PBM incentives are not aligned with minimizing net drug costs.

Biosimilar manufacturers, facing this environment, have developed their own response. Several biosimilar entrants now offer two versions of the same product at different list prices: a low-WAC version with minimal rebates, aimed at entities that make decisions based on net cost, and a high-WAC version with substantial rebates, aimed at PBMs that benefit from rebate revenue. This is the dual WAC strategy, and its existence is the clearest evidence that the biosimilar market is not functioning the way the generic market does.

In a market where competition works normally, manufacturers do not need to offer the same drug at two prices to serve different intermediary preferences. The dual WAC strategy is an accommodation to a broken incentive structure. It adds cost and complexity, fragments the biosimilar manufacturer's market, and means that the same product is sometimes priced higher specifically to generate the rebates that secure formulary access. Patients whose plans adopt the high-WAC version pay more in cost-sharing than patients whose plans adopt the low-WAC version, for the same drug, with the difference captured by the intermediary.

The welfare implications of this structure are worth tracing carefully. Rebates are often described as savings that reduce net spending for payers. But rebate savings are not automatically passed through to patients. Plan premiums reflect payer spending in aggregate, not drug-by-drug, and patient cost-sharing is often tied to list prices rather than net prices. A patient with a 20% coinsurance requirement pays 20% of the WAC, not 20% of the WAC minus the rebate their insurer collected. The rebate system can simultaneously lower what a plan spends on a drug and raise what the patient pays for it, with the difference as revenue for the PBM.

A useful framework for modeling this market is a multi-stage game between the originator, biosimilar entrants, and the payer. The originator moves first, setting a pricing strategy that incorporates anticipated biosimilar entry. Potential biosimilar entrants then decide whether to pay the substantial fixed cost of entry, on the order of $100-250 million, given expected post-entry competition and the originator's strategy. Entered biosimilars then set prices. The payer's influence operates throughout, shaping demand via formulary decisions that respond to manufacturer contracting.

The equilibrium predictions from this framework align with observed behavior. When perceived differentiation between the originator and biosimilars is low, or when payer pressure is high, the originator's optimal strategy is to compete aggressively on net price rather than cede the market. This is the "fight" outcome, characterized by large rebates and maintained list prices. When differentiation is perceived as high and payer pressure is low, the originator may "acquiesce," maintaining margins and accepting some volume loss to biosimilars. The transition between these equilibria depends critically on the size of the loyal segment of the market that will not switch to a biosimilar regardless of price.

Entry decisions by biosimilar firms are sensitive to expected post-entry profitability, which declines as more competitors enter. There is a level of competition beyond which additional entry is not profitable given the fixed costs involved. This generates a theoretically predicted "biosimilar void": for biologics where expected market size and post-entry margins are insufficient to justify development costs, no biosimilars will be developed even after the originator's patents expire. IQVIA has documented that for 106 of 118 biologics losing patent protection between 2025 and 2034, no biosimilars are currently in development. The market is growing in aggregate while large gaps persist for individual products.

The Inflation Reduction Act introduced two provisions directly relevant to this market. The first empowers Medicare to negotiate a maximum fair price for high-expenditure biologics that lack generic or biosimilar competition after a period of market exclusivity. The second temporarily increases Medicare Part B reimbursement for qualifying biosimilars from 6% to 8% of the reference product's ASP, intended to make prescribing biosimilars more financially attractive to physicians and hospitals.

The price negotiation provision creates a tension the framework makes visible. Negotiating a lower originator price shrinks the price umbrella under which biosimilar manufacturers compete. Since biosimilars price at a discount to the originator, a lower negotiated originator price means lower absolute margins for biosimilars, reducing the profitability of entry. For biologics already at the margin of attracting biosimilar development, price negotiation may push expected profits below the entry cost threshold, worsening the void. The policy lowers current costs while potentially reducing future competition. The net welfare effect depends on the magnitude of immediate savings relative to the value of foregone competition, a calculation that requires knowing how many biosimilar entries would have occurred in the counterfactual.

The Part B reimbursement enhancement addresses a specific distortion in the physician-administered drug market. Under standard Medicare Part B rules, physicians and hospitals are reimbursed at ASP plus 6% for drugs they administer. For a high-ASP originator, the 6% add-on is a larger absolute dollar amount than for a lower-ASP biosimilar, creating a financial incentive to use the more expensive product. The IRA's increase to ASP plus 8% of the reference product's ASP for qualifying biosimilars attempts to offset this. Early evidence suggests the impact has been modest, partly because the conditionality requires the biosimilar's ASP to be below the reference product's ASP, which aggressive originator net price competition can undermine, and partly because formulary and health system factors dominate individual prescribing decisions.

State-level Prescription Drug Affordability Boards add another layer. Several states have established boards with the authority to review drug affordability and impose upper payment limits on certain high-cost products within state-regulated markets. As binding price ceilings, these limits reduce manufacturer margins in the affected segment and strengthen the payer's negotiating position. The risks mirror those of federal negotiation: reduced margins can deter biosimilar development and, in the most aggressive scenarios, prompt manufacturers to restrict availability in the affected market. The effectiveness of these limits also depends heavily on design. A board that targets drugs without existing competition may affect different manufacturers and incentives than one that applies to a market where multiple competitors already exist.

The distributional consequences of these policy interventions are not neutral across stakeholders. Price controls and enhanced competition both reduce manufacturer surplus, particularly for originators. Payer spending falls. Whether these gains translate to lower costs for patients depends on whether savings are passed through via premiums and cost-sharing design, which is not guaranteed given the rebate structure. Policies that reduce biosimilar entry also harm future patients who would have benefited from competition in markets that never develop it.

The core tension in this market is that the mechanisms most effective at lowering current prices tend to weaken the conditions for sustained future competition. High biosimilar entry costs and the dependence of entry decisions on expected post-entry margins mean that the market equilibrium is fragile in a way the generic market is not. A generic manufacturer's entry cost is low enough that even modest margins attract competition. A biosimilar manufacturer's entry cost is high enough that competition is contingent on a sufficiently large price umbrella, which every cost-control measure compresses.

The biosimilar market is not failing in the trivial sense of having too few competitors. It is failing in the more interesting sense that its competitive mechanism is captured by an intermediary structure with misaligned incentives, and the policies designed to correct for this capture interact in ways that may trade near-term savings against long-run competition. Fixing it requires either reforming the rebate system so that PBM incentives align with minimizing net costs, or accepting that a market with these structural features will need continuous regulatory intervention to produce outcomes that transparent competition would deliver automatically.
