The Agent Problem

A pharmacy benefit manager serves two principals simultaneously. On one side are the employers and health plans that hire the PBM to administer their drug benefit, negotiate rebates with manufacturers, and design formularies. On the other side are the pharmaceutical manufacturers whose drugs must secure formulary placement to reach patients. The PBM manages this relationship for a fee, but its compensation is also tied, in ways often not fully transparent to either principal, to the rebates it extracts and retains. This makes the PBM neither a pure agent of the payer nor a pure agent of the manufacturer. It is something in between, and that ambiguity is the central structural fact of the US pharmaceutical distribution system.

The conventional critique of PBMs focuses on opacity and self-dealing: that PBMs retain rebates rather than passing them to payers, that they favor higher-list-price drugs because larger rebates generate more retained revenue, that their ownership of specialty pharmacies creates conflicts in dispensing decisions. These complaints are well-documented empirically. But the theoretical structure underlying them is more interesting than the conduct claims alone suggest, because it explains why the problem persists even when contracts are disclosed and fiduciary duties are nominally enforced.

The common agency literature in economics describes what happens when a single agent contracts with multiple principals who each want to influence the agent's behavior. A key prediction of this literature is that each principal free-rides on the incentives offered by the others. If insurer A offers the PBM strong performance incentives to minimize net drug costs, and insurer B does the same, each insurer bears the full cost of its incentive while capturing only part of the resulting benefit, since the PBM serves both and its improved performance accrues to each. The rational response for each insurer is to offer weaker incentives than would be optimal if it were the PBM's only client. The equilibrium is a collective underinvestment in PBM performance incentives, which helps explain why fee-for-service contracting with PBMs has proven durable despite well-understood inefficiencies.

The vertical integration of a PBM with a major insurer is, from this theoretical perspective, a direct solution to the common agency problem. When the PBM and insurer are the same firm, the free-riding problem disappears: the integrated entity captures the full benefit of its own performance incentives. The conflict of interest between the PBM's retained rebates and the insurer's interest in lower net costs is resolved by collapsing them into a single objective function. This is the efficiency argument for PBM-insurer integration, and it is coherent as far as it goes.

The problem is that resolving the common agency problem inside the integrated firm creates a different and arguably worse problem outside it. A standalone PBM that serves many clients has limited ability to credibly threaten to exclude a major drug from its formulary: exclusion would disadvantage all its clients simultaneously, many of whom might push back, and the PBM's leverage against any single manufacturer is therefore bounded. An integrated PBM-insurer has a more credible exclusion threat because its insurance arm can absorb the downstream consequences of exclusion more directly, steering affected patients toward alternative therapies within the integrated network or accepting the enrollment effects of a narrower formulary in exchange for higher rebates.

The mechanism here runs through what bargaining theory calls the disagreement payoff: the value a party receives if negotiations fail. When a standalone PBM and a manufacturer fail to reach agreement on a drug's formulary placement, the PBM must explain to its many clients why a major therapy is unavailable. This is costly, and the cost limits the PBM's willingness to walk away. When an integrated PBM-insurer fails to reach agreement with a manufacturer, its insurance arm has already priced the formulary, prepared alternative pathways for affected patients, and potentially benefits from the additional medical services its provider affiliates will deliver to patients who switch therapies. The integrated firm's disagreement payoff is higher, its exclusion threat is more credible, and manufacturers facing this negotiating position must offer larger rebates to avoid the risk.

This is a legitimate source of buyer power, and lower net acquisition costs for drugs are, in principle, a benefit. The question is who captures the difference between the old net price and the new one. If the integrated firm passes the rebate savings through to employers and patients in the form of lower premiums or reduced cost-sharing, the consolidation has achieved what its proponents claim. If the integrated firm retains the savings as profit while maintaining premium levels, the efficiency argument dissolves into a transfer from manufacturers to the integrated entity, with no benefit to patients. The pass-through rate is not determined by the logic of integration; it depends on downstream competition in the insurance market. In markets with two or three dominant insurer-PBM combinations, the competitive pressure to pass savings through is limited.

The more structurally novel consequence of PBM-insurer integration concerns its effect on rival insurers who remain dependent on the integrated firm's PBM services. Most regional employers and smaller health plans do not have the scale to operate their own PBM or to negotiate directly with manufacturers. They purchase PBM services from one of the large integrated entities, which means their formulary design and rebate negotiations are managed by a firm that also owns a competing health plan.

Economic theory predicts that this creates an incentive for input foreclosure. The integrated PBM, when designing formulary-contingent rebate contracts with manufacturers, internalizes the effect that those contracts have on its own insurance arm's profitability. If a particular formulary arrangement attracts favorable manufacturer rebates but also makes the PBM's rival health plan clients more cost-competitive, the integrated firm has reason to offer those clients less favorable arrangements, higher retained rebate rates, less aggressive negotiating on their behalf, or formulary designs that favor the integrated insurer's own products. The integrated PBM serves two principals again, but now one of them is itself.

This form of foreclosure is subtle and hard to detect directly. The rival insurer cannot easily observe whether the rebates it receives on a given drug represent the PBM's best effort or a deliberately softened negotiating outcome designed to preserve the integrated insurer's competitive advantage. Contract terms are complex and multidimensional. The PBM can attribute any shortfall in rebate performance to market conditions or manufacturer intransigence. The structural incentive to disadvantage rivals through the PBM relationship exists regardless of whether it is acted upon in any particular negotiation, and its mere existence changes the competitive dynamics of the insurance market in ways that price analysis alone will not capture.

There is a regulatory wrinkle here that has received insufficient theoretical attention. The Medical Loss Ratio requirement, established under the Affordable Care Act, requires that insurers spend at least 80% to 85% of premium revenue on clinical services and quality improvement, with the remainder available for administrative costs and profit. The intent is to limit insurer profitability by constraining the share of premium dollars that can be retained as overhead or returned to shareholders.

Vertical integration creates a straightforward mechanism for circumventing this constraint. An integrated insurer can make internal payments to its affiliated PBM or pharmacy subsidiary for services rendered. If these payments are classified as clinical expenditures or quality-related costs, they count toward the MLR requirement, inflating the numerator without delivering additional value to patients. The insurer meets its MLR threshold through what is effectively a transfer to itself, while retaining more of the premium as profit within the corporate family than the MLR rule intends to permit.

This is not a marginal or theoretical concern. The gap between what a vertically integrated firm charges its insurance arm for PBM or pharmacy services and what those services would cost on the open market is, by definition, not observable to regulators who rely on the insurer's internal accounting. Transfer pricing between affiliates is audited in standard corporate contexts, but the MLR framework was designed for an era when insurers purchased most services at arm's length. The standard tools for detecting MLR manipulation assume that high administrative costs reflect genuinely excessive overhead, not strategic transfers to affiliated entities that are not required to disclose their cost structures.

The theoretical prediction here is specific: vertical integration that captures PBM and pharmacy functions within an insurance holding company weakens the MLR constraint without visibly violating it. The insurer reports spending that satisfies the threshold; the spending flows to affiliates rather than to clinical services for patients; patients see no improvement in benefits and no reduction in premiums. The efficiency argument for integration, which rests on aligning incentives and reducing administrative friction, does not obviously explain why the savings from these efficiencies are not reflected in lower premiums or richer benefits. MLR circumvention offers an alternative explanation.

The horizontal consolidation of PBMs compounds these dynamics. The top three PBMs process the large majority of US prescription drug claims, and all three are vertically integrated with major insurers and pharmacy chains. This means that the market for PBM services, which smaller health plans and employers depend on, is simultaneously highly concentrated and dominated by entities with structural conflicts of interest relative to their clients.

High concentration in a market with complex, multidimensional products creates conditions for tacit collusion that are qualitatively different from those in simpler oligopoly settings. The relevant product here is not a commodity whose price can be observed and matched. It is a bundle of formulary management, rebate negotiation, specialty pharmacy, and utilization management services whose quality is difficult for clients to assess and whose pricing is embedded in confidential contracts. Mutual forbearance in such a market does not look like parallel pricing on a visible product. It looks like a collective absence of aggressive competition for clients on the dimensions that clients care most about, namely pass-through rates, net drug costs, and formulary breadth.

The multimarket contact literature in industrial organization provides a theoretical basis for this concern. When the same set of firms compete against each other across multiple markets, each firm's incentive to compete aggressively in any single market is dampened by the knowledge that aggression could trigger retaliation across all the others. The three large integrated PBM-insurer-pharmacy combinations compete against each other not just in the PBM services market but simultaneously in insurance markets across most major metropolitan areas, in specialty pharmacy distribution, and in Medicare Part D plan design. The scope for mutual forbearance across these dimensions is substantial. Theoretical models predict that this kind of multimarket contact leads to higher prices and lower quality across affected markets, and there is empirical evidence consistent with this prediction in Medicare Advantage markets where the same firms compete in many geographic areas simultaneously.

The implications for pharmaceutical access run through both the formulary channel and the provider network channel. On the formulary side, the theoretical concern is not that the integrated PBM will categorically exclude effective drugs, but that its formulary decisions will reflect a mix of clinical evidence, manufacturer rebates, and strategic considerations related to the integrated firm's downstream interests that are not visible to the patients and physicians affected. A drug that would receive favorable formulary placement under a competitive PBM market might receive less favorable placement if the manufacturer has refused to offer rebates large enough to compensate for the integrated firm's increased leverage, or if the drug competes with a therapy associated with the integrated network's owned specialty pharmacy or provider affiliates.

On the provider network side, vertical integration between insurers and hospital systems or physician groups creates analogous steering incentives. The integrated payer has financial reasons to direct patients toward its owned providers, and its network design choices can create access barriers to non-affiliated providers that are not formally exclusionary but that impose significant friction on patients seeking non-integrated care. For pharmaceutical access specifically, this matters when drug administration occurs in provider settings, as with infused therapies and increasingly with complex oral regimens requiring clinical monitoring. An integrated system with owned specialty pharmacies, infusion centers, and oncology practices has the ability to manage the entire care pathway in ways that favor its own economic interests, with formulary and referral decisions reinforcing each other.

The theoretical literature on consolidation generally frames the policy question as a trade-off between efficiency gains and market power risks, leaving the resolution as an empirical matter to be determined case by case. This framing is appropriate for mergers that combine previously independent firms in markets where the efficiency and power effects are genuinely uncertain. It is less well-suited to evaluating a market structure that has already consolidated substantially and where the theoretical mechanisms of harm are reasonably well-specified.

The PBM market has three dominant integrated players. The common agency inefficiency that motivated consolidation has been largely resolved within these firms at the cost of creating structural conflicts of interest in their PBM-to-client relationships. The MLR constraint has been weakened by the opacity of intra-corporate transfers. Multimarket contact among the three large integrated firms creates conditions conducive to tacit collusion across insurance, PBM, and pharmacy markets simultaneously. These are not speculative harms requiring further study. They are the predicted equilibrium consequences of the market structure that currently exists.

The regulatory response has focused primarily on transparency requirements: mandating disclosure of rebate pass-through rates, requiring PBMs to report spread pricing, strengthening fiduciary standards in PBM-client relationships. These measures address the conduct symptoms without changing the structural incentives that generate them. A PBM that is required to disclose its retained rebate rate is still a PBM with an incentive to maximize retained rebates. An integrated PBM-insurer that must report intra-corporate transfers in more detail is still an integrated entity with the ability to structure those transfers in ways that satisfy reporting requirements while preserving the underlying advantage.

The structural argument suggests a different priority ordering. The common agency problem that made standalone PBMs inefficient was real, but it could have been addressed by requiring full rebate pass-through rather than by permitting vertical integration that resolves the agency problem while creating foreclosure risks. The MLR circumvention concern could be addressed by excluding intra-corporate transfers to non-clinical affiliates from the MLR numerator. The multimarket contact concern is harder to address without structural remedies, which is precisely why it receives less regulatory attention despite being, theoretically, the most durable source of the problem.
